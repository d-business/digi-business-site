Ask ten agencies how much SEO costs and you'll get ten different numbers. That's not because SEO pricing is some dark art. It's rules-based, like everything else in marketing. The number moves for specific, knowable reasons: your industry, your competition, the state of your website.

Here's the honest headline. Most UK businesses pay between £500 and £5,000 a month. I could stop there and let you leave with a figure. 

But a number with no reasoning behind it is worthless. You'd have no way to tell if your quote is fair or if someone's taking you for a ride. So let's break down what actually drives that price: model by model, business by business.


## The short answer

Most UK businesses pay between £500 and £5,000 a month for SEO. Below that, you're probably not getting real work done. Above it, you're paying for scale: more content, harder keywords, bigger sites.

Where you land depends on one thing more than any other: how much competition you're up against for the search terms that matter to your business. A sole trader chasing local search pays a fraction of what a national retailer competing for "seo services" needs to spend.

## How SEO pricing models work

There are three ways agencies charge for SEO. Most businesses only ever hear about one of them and assume it's the only option.


### Monthly retainer

This is the model you'll meet most often. £500 to £5,000 a month, ongoing.

It works because SEO isn't a task you finish. It's a habit you keep up. Search results shift, competitors publish new content, Google changes how it ranks things. A retainer pays for someone watching all of that on your behalf, not just doing one job and walking away.

Good for: businesses who want steady, compounding progress and don't want to manage the work themselves.


### Hourly consulting

£30 to £150 an hour, sometimes more for specialists.

This suits a narrower need. You've got a specific problem, or you want a second opinion on what your current agency's doing, or you need someone to train your team. You're paying for expertise in the room, not a running campaign.

Good for: businesses with some in-house capability who need direction, not delivery.


### Project or one-off work

£1,000 to £10,000+, scoped and finished.

Technical audits, site migrations, a batch of content, a one-time cleanup after a penalty. These have a clear start and end, so they get priced as a fixed project rather than a monthly fee.

Good for: a defined piece of work with a defined outcome. Not a substitute for ongoing SEO if your goal is sustained ranking growth.

None of these is "better." They answer different questions. The mistake I see most often is a business signing a retainer when what they actually needed was one audit, or buying a one-off project when what they needed was an ongoing habit.

## Why prices vary so much

Same business size, same rough budget, wildly different quotes. Here's why.

**Industry competitiveness.** Some markets have ten agencies fighting over the same page one spot. Others have almost nobody competing at all. A personal injury solicitor in London is fighting a war. A regional joinery firm isn't. The harder the fight, the more hours it takes to win, and the higher the price.

**Site size and technical debt.** A five-page brochure site and a five-thousand-page ecommerce catalogue are not the same job. Neither are a clean, modern site and one built on ten-year-old code with broken redirects everywhere. You're not just paying for new work. Sometimes you're paying to undo old mistakes first.

**National versus local scope.** Ranking in one town is a smaller job than ranking across the whole UK. More locations, more competitors, more content needed to cover them all. Scope drives cost more directly than almost anything else on this list.

**Who's doing the work.** A freelancer, a small agency, and a large agency aren't pricing the same overheads. Freelancers are usually cheapest and most hands-on, but there's no cover if they're ill or busy. Large agencies carry more infrastructure and specialists, which shows up in the invoice. In-house hires cost a salary whether or not there's enough work to fill their week.

None of this is arbitrary. Ask why a quote is what it is, and a straight answer should point to one of these four things. If it doesn't, that's worth noticing.


## What changes the price for vehicle & leisure dealership sites

Everything above applies to any business. This part doesn't. If you run a caravan, motorhome, or marine dealership, three things push your SEO cost above what a similarly sized business in most other industries would pay.

**Stock feed SEO.** Your website probably has hundreds or thousands of individual vehicle pages, pulled from a stock feed that changes weekly. Vehicles get sold, new ones arrive, listings need to update without leaving a trail of dead pages or thin duplicate content behind. 

Most agencies quoting you have never managed a feed like this. They'll price you like a five-hundred-page brochure site and then discover the real job halfway through.

**The show calendar.** Caravan and motorhome buying isn't spread evenly across the year. It clusters around the NEC Show, SIAB, and new model year launches. Content and campaigns need to be timed to that rhythm, not scheduled like a generic twelve-month plan. Miss the seasonal window and you've missed the buyer.

**Multi-site dealer groups.** If you're running more than one showroom, each location needs its own Google Business Profile, its own local signals, its own coordination so one site doesn't cannibalise another's search visibility. That's more moving parts than a single-location business, and it should be priced as such.

None of this makes a dealership website expensive to work on by accident. It's expensive because the scope is genuinely bigger than a generic small business site, whatever the vehicle count or turnover suggests on paper. 

An agency that treats a caravan dealership like a car dealership, or a car dealership like any other local business, will underquote the job and then underdeliver on it.

## The real cost of cheap SEO

I had a prospect once, sat across the table, telling me my pricing was too high. "I could just go to Fiverr and buy a load of backlinks really cheaply compared to what you charge," he said.

"You could," I told him. "But when you came to me asking to unpick all those links from Russian porn sites, I think you'd regret paying for those cheap links."

He didn't buy the Fiverr links.

That's what cheap SEO usually is. Not a discount version of the same work. A different, riskier product wearing the same name. Under about £500 a month, you're rarely paying for genuine strategy and content. You're paying for volume: mass-produced links from low-quality or outright toxic sites, thin AI-generated content, automated tactics that chase a quick ranking bump.

Google notices. When it does, you don't just lose the ranking gain. You lose ground you already had. A manual penalty can take six to eighteen months to recover from, and the cleanup, the audit, the disavow work, the content rebuild, typically costs £5,000 to £15,000. That's not a worst-case scenario. That's the standard bill for undoing cheap SEO done badly.

Marketing is an asset, not an expense. Buy the cheap version and you're not saving money. You're borrowing against a much larger bill, due later, with interest.


## How to budget for SEO sensibly

Forget the number for a second. Before you agree to any figure, ask the agency these questions. The answers matter more than the price itself.



* **What are we trying to achieve, not just do?** If the answer is a list of tasks (content, links, audits) with no business outcome attached, that's a tactics-first pitch. Objectives come first, then strategy, then tactics. Never the other way round.
* **What does success look like in six months, not six weeks?** SEO compounds. Anyone promising fast results is either inexperienced or cutting corners you'll pay for later.
* **What's included in the fee, specifically?** Ask for the line items from the section above. If they can't tell you, they may not know themselves.
* **Can I see proof of past work?** Not testimonials. Actual before-and-after data, from a business roughly your size and situation.
* **What happens if my industry or site is more complex than average?** This tells you whether the quote already accounts for the "why prices vary" factors above, or whether it's a flat rate that'll need renegotiating in month two.

A sensible budget isn't the cheapest number you can find. It's the number that matches what you're actually asking for. Get that wrong and you'll either overpay for work you didn't need or underpay for work that never had a chance of doing the job.

## FAQ

**How much does SEO cost per month in the UK?** Most businesses pay between £500 and £5,000 a month, depending on your industry, site size, and how much competition you're facing. See the table earlier in this post for a breakdown by business size.

**How much does SEO cost per hour in the UK?** Hourly consulting runs £30 to £150 an hour, sometimes more for specialists. This suits a specific problem or a second opinion, not an ongoing campaign.

**How much does SEO cost for a small business in the UK?** Typically £500 to £2,000 a month. That covers technical fixes, on-page work, and regular content. Sole traders and very local businesses often pay less, from around £300 a month.

**Is SEO worth paying for?** If you treat it as an asset rather than an expense, yes. It compounds. Cheap SEO isn't a worthwhile shortcut. It's a different, riskier product that usually costs more to fix than it saves.

**What's included in a typical SEO package?** A technical audit, ongoing content creation, link building or digital PR, and the strategy and reporting time that decides what happens next and proves whether it's working. What's actually included varies, which is exactly why you should ask.
