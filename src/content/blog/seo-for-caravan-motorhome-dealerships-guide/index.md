Every guide to marketing a caravan dealership says the same thing. Build your content through spring. Peak in July and August. Sell the dream, not the vehicle.

Check the numbers before you believe it. Search interest for "caravans for sale" spiked in March 2026, hard enough to beat the whole of last year's summer peak in a single month.

It didn't fall back either. It sat high right through the summer, instead of building to one clean August peak the way it always has.

Nobody selling SEO advice to this sector has looked at that. What's out there falls into two camps.

Car dealer SEO with "caravan" bolted on. Or lifestyle content that tells you to sell the adventure and stops there. Neither mentions the stock feed duplicating your pages, the page speed your image galleries are killing, or the site structure that falls apart the moment you run more than one location or brand.

This guide is the other half. The data behind why the old assumptions are already wrong, and the technical groundwork none of that content can rank without.

That's UK search interest for "caravans for sale," month by month, for the last three years. In 2024 and 2025 it climbs steadily to an August peak, exactly as you'd expect. In 2026 it spikes early, and instead of building further to one clean summer high, it sits in an elevated band from February right through July.

Before reading anything into that, we checked the obvious explanation. [The Caravan, Camping & Motorhome Show](https://ccmshow.co.uk/) runs at the NEC every February: 13–18 in 2024, 18–23 in 2025, 17–22 in 2026. Same timing every year. If the show explained the spike, we'd see it every year. We don't. That rules out the simplest answer.

It still needs caveats, and we're not skipping them for the sake of a tidier story. This is one year of data. Search interest measures relative attention, not confirmed sales. And it's specific to caravans: run the same check on "motorhomes for sale" or "campervans for sale" and nothing unusual shows up. Whatever's happening here hasn't happened to the rest of the sector, at least not yet.

Compare that to the trade press. One report has motorhome registrations up over 20% last year. Another has the wider market down around 7%. A third describes dealers closing and shifting to storage. 

The [National Caravan Council](http://www.thencc.org.uk/) holds the actual registration data, and it's locked behind membership access. Three secondary sources, three different stories, and no way to check the primary number ourselves.

That's not a criticism of any of those reports. It's the reason to look at real search behaviour directly, instead of picking whichever headline suits the argument you already wanted to make.

For marketing budgets, the honest takeaway is patience over reaction. A market that can't agree on its own direction, and that might be shifting its buying calendar underneath everyone's feet, rewards SEO built on evidence rather than whichever number or seasonal assumption happens to be fashionable this quarter.

Which is exactly why the next question matters. Does the advice already out there for this sector actually fit, or has everyone just borrowed it from somewhere else?


## Why Generic Automotive SEO Doesn't Fit This Sector

Car dealer SEO is built for short decisions. Someone needs a car this month, searches by price and model, and buys within weeks.

Caravan and motorhome buyers don't work that way. They spend months, sometimes over a year, reading reviews, comparing layouts, and watching video walkthroughs.

Most of the trade already knows this. It's the one thing every lifestyle marketing guide gets right.

But knowing your customer takes longer to decide isn't a strategy. It's a starting point.

The real problem sits underneath the content. Most dealerships in this sector don't sell one brand from one site.

They run multiple locations, multiple manufacturer franchises (Burstner, Carado, Carthago and the rest), sometimes several separate domains for each brand.

Car dealer SEO guides never touch this. They're written for a single forecourt selling one type of stock.

Add three sites, five brands and overlapping stock feeds, and the technical problems multiply fast. Duplicate pages. Competing URLs. Search engines that can't work out which page should rank for which model.

That's the gap this guide actually closes.


## The Technical Foundations Nobody's Written About

Every guide to this sector talks about content. None of them talk about the site underneath it. That's the gap.


### Stock Feed and Inventory SEO

Most dealerships don't write their own stock listings. They pull from a manufacturer feed or a dealer management system, and that same feed often goes out to several sites at once.

To Google, identical descriptions across multiple domains look like duplicate content. Nobody's page looks unique enough to earn the ranking.

Three things fix this. Canonical tags that point Google to the one page that should rank. Model descriptions rewritten in your own words, not the manufacturer's copy-paste. And the harder call: deciding when a thin, near-identical stock page shouldn't be indexed at all, rather than letting hundreds of low-value pages dilute the site.

### Site Architecture for Multi-Site and Franchise Groups

Run one site selling one brand and structure barely matters. Run five sites across three brands and several locations, and it decides whether you rank at all.

The usual mistake: every location page optimised for the same phrase. "Motorhome dealer Yorkshire" showing up on three of your own sites isn't three chances to rank. It's your own pages competing with each other, and Google picking one and burying the rest.

The fix is a clear hierarchy. One page per brand, one per location, one per model, each targeting a distinct phrase, all linked so authority flows between them instead of splitting.


### Core Web Vitals and Page Speed

Vehicle galleries sell caravans and motorhomes. Done badly, they also wreck a site's speed.

High-resolution interior shots, 360 tours, walkthrough videos. All essential to a buyer deciding from their sofa. All capable of dragging load times past the point Google, and the buyer, will tolerate.

The fix isn't fewer images. It's compression, lazy loading so only visible images load first, and hosting video off-site through YouTube or Vimeo rather than serving huge files directly. Core Web Vitals are a ranking factor. They're also, more bluntly, the reason a buyer gives up and rings a competitor instead.


### Structured Data and Schema

Generic SEO advice says "add schema." It rarely says which.

A vehicle listing needs vehicle-specific schema: make, model, price, mileage, berths, condition. That's what earns rich results, and what tells search engines exactly what's for sale rather than making them guess from a page of prose.

Multi-site groups need local business schema on every single location too, not just one for the group. Miss this and one site quietly wins the local pack while the others stay invisible, even though they're selling the same stock.

## Local SEO and Google Business Profile for Multi-Site Groups

Every dealership needs a Google Business Profile. Multi-site groups need one done properly for every location, not one for the group and an afterthought for the rest.

Each site needs its own profile, its own reviews, and its own local landing page, rather than a shared page trying to represent three postcodes at once. Get this wrong and one location dominates local search while the others barely show up, even with identical stock.

This deserves more space than a guide covering the whole of SEO can give it.

## Content That Matches How Buyers Actually Search

Ask around and you'll hear the same number: caravan and motorhome buyers spend six to eighteen months deciding. It's repeated everywhere in this sector's marketing advice.

Nobody seems to know where that number came from. Treat it as a working assumption, not a verified fact. What the data does confirm is the shape of it: buyers don't decide overnight, and the searches they run change as they get closer to buying.

Split your content around three real stages.

Early research is informational. People comparing caravans against motorhomes, working out running costs, deciding whether this lifestyle suits them at all. This content earns attention, not enquiries.

Comparison and shortlisting narrows things down. Specific models, specific layouts, specific brands set against each other.

Ready to buy shows up clearly in the data. Searches like "caravan dealer near me" carry strong commercial intent, confirmed rather than assumed. This is where your stock pages and location pages need to do the heavy lifting, because the buyer has already decided what they want and is choosing who to buy it from.

Here's where the March spike from earlier in this guide actually matters. If touring caravan buyers are researching in February and March instead of building steadily to a July peak, then a dealership publishing "get your caravan ready for summer" content in May is publishing it to an audience that's already moved on.

That's worth reviewing, not tearing up. Bring your caravan-specific content calendar forward ahead of the usual spring ramp-up. Leave the motorhome and campervan calendar exactly where it is. Neither term showed the same shift, and there's no evidence yet that they need to.


## Reviews and Trust Signals

Reviews matter everywhere. They matter more here.

Caravan and motorhome owners talk to each other. Forums, Facebook groups, club meets and rallies all carry more weight than most industries get from word of mouth. A bad review doesn't just cost you a search ranking. It gets discussed.

Ask for reviews after the sale, not just after the visit. Respond to every one, good or bad, and respond properly rather than with a copy-pasted line. A short, specific reply to a genuine complaint does more for trust than a hundred five-star ratings with no responses attached.

This isn't complicated. It's just easy to neglect.


## How to Measure Whether Any of This Is Working

Don't expect a traffic graph to prove this guide worked. Not for months, possibly not for a year.

"SEO for caravan dealerships" has next to no measurable search volume. A page built to own that phrase can rank position one and still bring in a handful of visits a month. Judging it by organic traffic alone misses the point entirely.

Watch Search Console instead. Impressions and average position for the niche phrases, even at near-zero volume, tell you whether the category is starting to get searched at all. Rising impressions before rising clicks is the early signal, not a failure to wait for.

The rest of the proof is qualitative. Trade press picking it up. Being shared on LinkedIn by people in the industry, not just by us. And the one that actually matters commercially: a prospect mentioning they read it, in a sales call, before you've said a word about it.

That's a slower kind of win. It's also the only honest one available here.


## Common Mistakes

**Copying a car dealer's website.** Different buyer, different journey, different site. A template built for someone who decides in a fortnight will fight you every time a buyer researching for months tries to use it.

**Ignoring stock feed duplication.** The manufacturer feed goes out to a dozen other dealers too. If your listings read identically to theirs, Google has no reason to rank yours above anyone else's.

**Letting multiple locations cannibalise each other.** Five sites all fighting for the same phrase isn't five chances to rank. It's one phrase split five ways, and Google settling the argument for you.

**Treating lifestyle content as a substitute for technical SEO, not a complement to it.** This is the one worth ending on. Sell the adventure all you like. If the site underneath it can't be crawled properly, loads slowly, and duplicates half its own stock, none of that content gets the chance to work. The lifestyle angle isn't wrong. It's just not enough on its own, and every guide that stops there is only telling you half the story.


## FAQ


### What is SEO for a caravan or motorhome dealership, specifically?

Making sure your site can be found by people searching to buy, without losing them to duplicate stock pages, slow image galleries, or a site structure fighting itself across multiple locations. Generic automotive SEO covers half of that. The buyer behaviour and technical structure specific to this sector are the other half.


### Can a small dealership do this without an agency?

Some of it, yes. Reviews, a properly filled-out Google Business Profile, and writing your own model descriptions instead of reusing the manufacturer's feed text are all within reach without outside help.

The technical side is harder to do well without experience. Stock feed deduplication, multi-location site architecture and structured data can make things worse if they're done wrong, not just fail to help.


### Is the motorhome/caravan market slowing down?

Depends who you ask, and that's the honest answer. Trade reports this year have claimed everything from a 20% rise in motorhome registrations to dealers closing and pivoting to storage. We look at Google's own search data on this earlier in this guide, and even that shows a genuinely mixed picture rather than a clean answer either way.


### How is SEO different for leisure vehicle dealers vs car dealers?

Slower buyer decisions, for a start. Multi-brand, multi-site franchise structures that create technical problems car dealer guides never touch. And a research phase that, per the data in this guide, may already be shifting earlier in the year for touring caravans specifically. Car dealer SEO advice is a reasonable starting point. It's not a finished strategy.
